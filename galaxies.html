Galaxies: Islands of Stars

Galaxies are vast cosmic structures containing billions to trillions of stars, interstellar gas, dust, and dark matter, all bound by gravity. They exhibit a stunning variety of shapes and sizes, from elegant spirals like our own Milky Way to massive ellipticals and chaotic irregulars. Explore some breathtaking examples captured through humanity's most powerful telescopes.

Pinwheel Galaxy (M101)

A grand design spiral galaxy famed for its well-defined arms and vast star-forming regions. This is one of the largest and most detailed galaxy portraits by Hubble.

Credit: NASA, ESA, K. Kuntz (JHU), F. Bresolin (U Hawaii), J. Trauger (JPL), J. Mould (NOAO), Y.-H. Chu (U Illinois), STScI
Andromeda Galaxy (M31)

Our closest large galactic neighbor, hurtling towards the Milky Way for an eventual cosmic collision billions of years from now. It contains over a trillion stars.

Credit: NASA, ESA, J. Dalcanton, B.F. Williams, L.C. Johnson (U Washington), PHAT team, R. Gendler
Antennae Galaxies (NGC 4038/4039)

A stunning example of galactic interaction. These two galaxies are deep in the process of merging, their collision triggering massive bursts of star formation.

Credit: NASA, ESA, and B. Whitmore (STScI); G. Trancho (Northern Arizona University)
Sombrero Galaxy (M104)

Known for its bright nucleus, large central bulge, and prominent dust lane, giving it the appearance of a sombrero hat. It's an unbarred spiral galaxy.

Credit: NASA/ESA and The Hubble Heritage Team (STScI/AURA)
Whirlpool Galaxy (M51)

A classic spiral galaxy interacting with its smaller companion, NGC 5195. Its distinct structure makes it a favorite target for astronomers studying galactic evolution.

Credit: NASA, ESA, S. Beckwith (STScI), and The Hubble Heritage Team (STScI/AURA)
Centaurus A (NGC 5128)

A peculiar galaxy, likely the result of a merger between an elliptical and a spiral galaxy. It's notable for its strong radio emissions powered by a supermassive black hole.

Credit: NASA, ESA, and the Hubble Heritage (STScI/AURA)-ESA/Hubble Collaboration
